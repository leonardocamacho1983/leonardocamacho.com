Thinking Standards: Internal Operating Rules

Purpose:
- Raise conceptual quality and decision usefulness.
- Keep writing operational, not academic.

Core discipline:
1. Separate three layers in every analysis:
- observed: what is directly visible in behavior, data, or decisions.
- inferred: what is concluded from patterns in observations.
- assumed: what is provisionally accepted to make the argument move.

2. Do not explain without mechanism.
- Every major claim must include a causal path.
- If causality is unclear, narrow the claim instead of filling with abstraction.

3. Do not collapse categories.
- Do not reduce system-level phenomena to individual intent.
- Do not reduce emergent phenomena to intentional decisions.
- Do not reduce perceptual limits to coordination failures.
- Do not reduce structural constraints to motivation problems.

4. Classify the dominant phenomenon before drafting:
- mechanical: direct process or rule interactions.
- structural: role, incentive, interface, or architecture effects.
- perceptual: visibility, interpretation, or sensemaking limits.
- emergent: aggregate outcomes no actor explicitly designed.

5. Resist false binaries.
- If evidence supports multiple mechanisms, keep that plurality explicit.
- Prefer bounded claims over totalizing claims.

6. Calibrate certainty.
- Do not overstate certainty.
- Prefer a precise limited claim over a broad inflated one.

7. Avoid false clarity.
- If the problem is hard to perceive in real operation, preserve that difficulty.
- Preserve complexity without becoming vague.

8. Force concrete language.
- Rewrite ambiguous abstractions into operational terms.
- Terms like alignment, coherence, local, and system must be concretized with actors, constraints, and consequences.

9. Keep reasoning stages distinct:
- diagnosis: what is happening.
- explanation: why it happens.
- implication: what follows for judgment.
- application: what action to take now.

10. Preserve conceptual fidelity.
- Do not simplify by flattening structure.
- Accessibility must come from clearer mechanism, not weaker ideas.

Execution checks:
- Can a reader identify observed vs inferred vs assumed elements?
- Is at least one mechanism explicit and testable?
- Is the phenomenon type explicit or inferable from the draft?
- Are category mistakes avoided?
- Are diagnosis, explanation, implication, and application all present and distinct?
